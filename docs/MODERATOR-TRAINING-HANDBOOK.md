# MODERATOR TRAINING HANDBOOK
**SEEN by CREOVA — Community Moderation Guide**

**Version:** 1.0  
**Date:** February 6, 2026  
**For:** Community, Institutional, and Platform Moderators

## INTRODUCTION

SEEN by CREOVA is not a social media platform. We do not have comment sections, user uploads, or algorithmic feeds. But we do have community engagement—through story submissions, educational use, and institutional partnerships.

Moderation at SEEN is about **cultural stewardship**, not content policing. Moderators are guides, not gatekeepers. Your role is to ensure content remains culturally grounded, historically accurate, and respectful of the communities it represents.

---

## CATEGORY 1: CULTURAL SENSITIVITY

### Definition

Cultural sensitivity means understanding the specific histories, contexts, and protocols of Black, Indigenous, and people of color communities in Canada. It means recognizing that not all BIPOC experiences are the same, and that language, representation, and framing matter deeply.

### Core Principles

**1. Context Matters**
- A word acceptable in one community may be harmful in another
- Historical terms must be contextualized, not censored
- Contemporary terminology evolves—stay informed

**2. Community Authority**
- Indigenous communities determine Indigenous representation
- Black communities determine Black representation
- Asian communities determine Asian representation
- Moderators facilitate, communities decide

**3. Avoid Flattening**
- "BIPOC" is useful shorthand, not a monolith
- Black Canadian ≠ Black American
- Indigenous ≠ a single culture (there are hundreds of nations)
- Asian diaspora includes dozens of distinct communities

### Examples

**Example 1: Terminology**

**Situation:** A user submits a story that uses the term "Indian" to describe Indigenous peoples.

**Decision:**
- **If historical context:** Keep the term but add context card explaining historical usage and contemporary preference for "Indigenous" or specific nation names (Cree, Mi'kmaq, etc.)
- **If contemporary use:** Flag for revision. Suggest updating to "Indigenous" or specific nation name.

**Rationale:** Historical documents may use outdated terms—we preserve them with context. Contemporary writing should reflect current preferences.

---

**Example 2: Black Diaspora Nuances**

**Situation:** A story conflates Black Canadian and Black American civil rights movements.

**Decision:**
- Flag for revision
- Explain: Canadian racism operated differently than US racism (no legal slavery post-1834, but systemic racism persisted)
- Suggest: Focus on Canadian-specific movements (Viola Desmond, Africville, etc.)

**Rationale:** SEEN centers Canadian stories. US comparisons are useful but Canadian history must lead.

---

**Example 3: Asian Diaspora Specificity**

**Situation:** A story uses "Asian" without specifying which Asian community (Chinese, Japanese, South Asian, Southeast Asian, etc.)

**Decision:**
- Flag for clarification
- Ask: Which specific community? Komagata Maru (South Asian), Japanese internment (Japanese Canadian), Chinese head tax (Chinese Canadian)?
- Suggest: Use specific terms where possible

**Rationale:** "Asian" is too broad. Specificity honors distinct histories.

---

### Decision Rules

**GREEN LIGHT (No Moderation Needed):**
- ✅ Story uses specific terminology (Cree, Haitian-Canadian, Japanese Canadian)
- ✅ Historical terms are contextualized
- ✅ Community voices are centered (not external commentary)

**YELLOW LIGHT (Request Revision):**
- ⚠️ Terminology is outdated but used without context
- ⚠️ Story conflates distinct communities
- ⚠️ Non-Canadian framing dominates

**RED LIGHT (Do Not Publish):**
- ❌ Slurs used without historical context or necessity
- ❌ Cultural appropriation (non-community member speaking for community)
- ❌ Harmful stereotypes presented as fact

### Escalation Path

**Unsure about cultural protocols?**
1. Pause—do not approve immediately
2. Consult community elder or cultural advisor
3. If still unsure, escalate to platform moderator
4. Document decision for future reference

**Remember:** It's better to delay approval than to publish harmful content.

## CATEGORY 2: HARM PREVENTION

### Definition

Harm prevention means recognizing content that could re-traumatize viewers, perpetuate stereotypes, or cause psychological harm—especially to marginalized communities who have experienced systemic violence.

### Core Principles

**1. Trauma-Informed Approach**
- Assume viewers may have experienced the harms depicted
- Content warnings are not censorship—they're care
- User control over exposure is essential

**2. Representation ≠ Glorification**
- Depicting racism to critique it is different from perpetuating it
- Depicting violence to document history is different from sensationalizing it
- Context and framing matter

**3. Dignity First**
- Even when documenting injustice, center dignity
- Avoid "poverty porn" or "trauma porn"
- Resistance and resilience should be visible, not just suffering

### Examples

**Example 1: Graphic Content**

**Situation:** A story includes graphic descriptions of residential school abuse.

**Decision:**
- **If necessary for truth-telling:** Allow, but require content warning
- **Content warning text:** "This story contains descriptions of physical and sexual abuse in residential schools. Viewer discretion advised."
- **User control:** Allow users to skip or navigate away without judgment

**Rationale:** Residential school history must be told, but survivors and descendants should control their exposure.

---

**Example 2: Racist Language (Historical Context)**

**Situation:** A story quotes historical documents that contain slurs.

**Decision:**
- **If direct quote from historical source:** Allow with context
- **Context required:** Explain the source, date, and why the quote is included
- **Alternative:** Use "[slur]" with explanation rather than full word if possible

**Rationale:** Historical accuracy is important, but gratuitous use of slurs is harmful.

---

**Example 3: Stereotypes**

**Situation:** A story portrays Black Canadians primarily as victims of racism, with no agency or resistance.

**Decision:**
- Flag for revision
- Suggest: Include stories of resistance, organizing, joy, success
- Explain: Centering only suffering perpetuates "Black people as victims" stereotype

**Rationale:** SEEN's mandate is to show full humanity—suffering AND resistance, trauma AND joy.

---

### Decision Rules

**Content Warnings Required:**
- Physical violence (police brutality, residential school abuse, hate crimes)
- Sexual violence
- Suicide or self-harm
- Graphic imagery (even if historical)
- Racist language (even if quoted historically)

**RED LIGHT (Do Not Publish):**
- ❌ Gratuitous violence (not necessary for story)
- ❌ Glorification of harm
- ❌ Sensationalized trauma ("poverty porn," "trauma porn")
- ❌ Dehumanizing portrayals

**Escalation Path:**
- If content is borderline, consult platform moderator
- If content involves active harm (ongoing abuse, danger), escalate immediately to CREOVA staff

## CATEGORY 3: ACCESSIBILITY & LANGUAGE

### Definition

Accessibility means ensuring all users—regardless of ability or language—can engage with SEEN content. This includes screen reader compatibility, caption availability, and multilingual access.

### Core Principles

**1. Accessibility Is Not Optional**
- WCAG 2.1 AA compliance is baseline
- Missing transcripts, captions, or translations are gaps, not preferences

**2. Language Equity**
- EN/FR/ES are equally important (not EN primary, others secondary)
- Content should be culturally fluent, not literal translations

**3. Technical Barriers = Exclusion**
- If a screen reader can't access content, it's not accessible
- If a film lacks captions, it's not accessible

### Examples

**Example 1: Missing Transcripts**

**Situation:** A film submitted for inclusion lacks a transcript.

**Decision:**
- Flag for completion
- Do not approve until transcript is provided
- Offer assistance: Direct submitter to transcription services (Rev.com, etc.)

**Rationale:** Transcripts are required for screen reader accessibility and WCAG compliance.

---

**Example 2: Machine Translation**

**Situation:** A story has been translated to French using Google Translate. The translation is grammatically correct but culturally awkward.

**Decision:**
- Flag for revision
- Explain: Machine translation misses cultural nuance
- Suggest: Human translator or bilingual community member review

**Rationale:** Language equity means culturally fluent translations, not robotic conversions.

---

**Example 3: Audio-Only Content Without Text**

**Situation:** A story world includes audio narration but no text alternative.

**Decision:**
- Flag as accessibility gap
- Require: Text version available for users who cannot access audio

**Rationale:** Deaf/hard-of-hearing users must have equal access.

---

### Decision Rules

**GREEN LIGHT (Accessible):**
- ✅ Text available for all audio content
- ✅ Captions/subtitles available for all video
- ✅ Screen reader compatible (proper HTML structure)
- ✅ Multilingual content culturally fluent

**YELLOW LIGHT (Accessibility Gaps):**
- ⚠️ Missing transcripts (but video has captions)
- ⚠️ Translation exists but needs cultural review
- ⚠️ Some content in single language only

**RED LIGHT (Not Accessible):**
- ❌ Audio-only content with no text alternative
- ❌ Video with no captions
- ❌ Content not screen reader compatible

**Escalation Path:**
- Flag technical accessibility issues to platform moderators
- Request translator support for multilingual gaps

## CATEGORY 4: RESTORATIVE COMMUNITY CARE

### Definition

Restorative community care means repairing harm, supporting affected parties, and maintaining healthy community dynamics. When conflicts arise, we prioritize healing over punishment.

### Core Principles

**1. Center Affected Parties**
- Those harmed by content/behavior determine the response
- Moderators facilitate, communities lead

**2. Repair > Punishment**
- Goal is restored relationships, not exclusion
- Accountability includes making amends, not just apologies

**3. Community Healing**
- Some conflicts require collective processing
- Create space for dialogue, not just top-down decisions

### Examples

**Example 1: Cultural Misrepresentation**

**Situation:** A non-Indigenous user submits a story about Indigenous communities that contains factual errors.

**Decision:**
- **Step 1:** Reach out to submitter privately (not publicly shaming)
- **Step 2:** Explain specific errors with sources
- **Step 3:** Offer opportunity to revise with community guidance
- **Step 4:** If submitter revises respectfully, publish. If not, decline with explanation.

**Rationale:** Assume good intent, educate first, exclude only if harm persists.

---

**Example 2: Community Conflict**

**Situation:** Two community members disagree about how a story should be told. Both have valid perspectives rooted in different community experiences.

**Decision:**
- **Step 1:** Acknowledge both perspectives are valid
- **Step 2:** Facilitate dialogue (not impose decision)
- **Step 3:** Explore compromise (e.g., include both perspectives, add context card)
- **Step 4:** If no agreement, escalate to broader community input

**Rationale:** SEEN is community-driven. Moderators mediate, not dictate.

---

**Example 3: Harm Caused by Published Content**

**Situation:** After publication, a community member reports that content caused harm (misrepresentation, triggering content without warning, etc.)

**Decision:**
- **Step 1:** Thank reporter, take concern seriously
- **Step 2:** Review content with affected party's input
- **Step 3:** If harm confirmed, options include:
  - Add content warning
  - Add context card with community perspective
  - Revise content with original creator's consent
  - Remove content if harm is severe and irreparable
- **Step 4:** Communicate decision transparently

**Rationale:** Content can cause unintended harm. Restorative approach means acknowledging, repairing, learning.

---

### Decision Rules

**Restorative Actions:**
- Private communication first (not public callouts)
- Education before exclusion
- Repair opportunities provided
- Community voice centered

**When to Remove Content:**
- Harm is severe and documented
- Creator refuses accountability
- Community consensus supports removal
- No restorative path forward

**Escalation Path:**
- Complex conflicts: Bring in external facilitator (restorative justice practitioner)
- Severe harm: Escalate to platform moderators immediately
- Legal concerns: Escalate to CREOVA staff legal team

## MODERATOR ROLES & RESPONSIBILITIES

### Community Moderators (6-8 people)

**Role:** Peer-led cultural gatekeeping

**Responsibilities:**
- Review user submissions (if feature enabled)
- Flag cultural concerns
- Provide community perspective
- Participate in decision-making

**Time Commitment:** 5-10 hours/month  
**Compensation:** $500/month stipend

**Requirements:**
- Member of BIPOC community
- Familiarity with SEEN content
- Completed training (12 hours)
- Commitment to restorative practices

---

### Institutional Moderators (2-3 people)

**Role:** Academic/institutional liaison

**Responsibilities:**
- Review educational content for accuracy
- Ensure citation standards
- Connect SEEN with universities/museums
- Provide scholarly perspective

**Time Commitment:** 3-5 hours/month  
**Compensation:** Volunteer or institutional partnership

**Requirements:**
- Academic or museum affiliation
- Expertise in BIPOC Canadian history
- Completed training (6 hours)

---

### Platform Moderators (2 CREOVA staff)

**Role:** Policy enforcement, technical escalation

**Responsibilities:**
- Final decisions on content disputes
- Technical troubleshooting
- Policy updates
- Legal compliance

**Time Commitment:** Full-time staff  
**Compensation:** Staff salary

**Requirements:**
- CREOVA employee
- Full training (12 hours)
- Legal/policy expertise

## CODE OF CONDUCT

**As a SEEN moderator, I commit to:**

1. **Cultural Humility:** I recognize I do not know everything about every community. I will listen, learn, and defer to community expertise.

2. **Confidentiality:** I will not share sensitive information (user submissions, private communications) outside moderation team.

3. **No Bias:** I will not use moderation power to advance personal agendas or silence perspectives I disagree with.

4. **Transparency:** I will document moderation decisions and explain reasoning when asked.

5. **Accountability:** I will accept feedback on my moderation and participate in ongoing training.

6. **Community Care:** I will prioritize community wellbeing over rigid rule enforcement.
